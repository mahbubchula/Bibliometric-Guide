1.1 What is Bibliometric Analysis?

Definition and Core Purpose

Bibliometrics is the quantitative analysis of written publications, particularly scientific articles. It uses statistical and mathematical methods to study patterns in scholarly communication, research productivity, and knowledge diffusion.

Key Definition:

"Bibliometrics is the application of mathematics and statistical methods to books and other media of communication." - Alan Pritchard (1969)

Why Use Bibliometrics?

  • Objectivity: Reduces subjective bias in literature reviews
  • Comprehensiveness: Analyzes thousands of papers systematically
  • Pattern Discovery: Reveals trends invisible to traditional reviews
  • Network Visualization: Shows connections between ideas, authors, and institutions
  • Evidence-Based: Provides quantitative evidence for research trends

Bibliometrics vs. Traditional Literature Review:

Aspect Traditional Review Bibliometric Analysis
Sample Size 50-100 papers 500-5000+ papers
Approach Qualitative, narrative Quantitative, statistical
Bias Author's interpretation Data-driven patterns
Time Required 3-6 months 2-4 weeks (with tools)
Best For Deep content analysis Mapping research landscape

1.2 Brief History of Bibliometrics

From Citation Indexing to Modern Science Mapping

Timeline of Key Developments:

1926: Alfred Lotka - Lotka's Law (author productivity distribution)

1934: Samuel Bradford - Bradford's Law (journal article distribution)

1948: George Zipf - Zipf's Law (word frequency distribution)

1955: Eugene Garfield - Founded Institute for Scientific Information (ISI)

1963: Science Citation Index (SCI) launched - revolutionary!

1969: Alan Pritchard - Coined the term "bibliometrics"

2004: Scopus database launched by Elsevier

2010s: R packages (bibliometrix), VOSviewer, CiteSpace developed

2020s: AI-assisted bibliometric analysis, real-time dashboards

üí° Fun Fact

Eugene Garfield's Science Citation Index was initially rejected by publishers who thought "nobody would ever want to trace citations." Today, it's the foundation of Web of Science!

1.3 Fundamental Concepts & Terminology

Essential vocabulary you need to know

1. Citation-Based Concepts:

üìå Citation Analysis:

Studying who cites whom. If Paper B cites Paper A, Paper A gains one citation.

  • Purpose: Measure impact and influence
  • Example: A paper with 500 citations is likely influential
  • Limitation: Citations take time to accumulate; recent papers underrepresented

üìå Co-Citation Analysis:

When two papers (A and B) are both cited by a third paper (C), they are "co-cited."

  • Purpose: Reveals intellectual structure of PAST research
  • Example: Two seminal papers on "deep learning" co-cited 1000+ times ‚Üí they form the field's foundation
  • Use Case: Identify foundational theories and classic papers

üìå Bibliographic Coupling:

When two papers (A and B) both cite the same third paper (C), they are "bibliographically coupled."

  • Purpose: Reveals thematic similarity of CURRENT research
  • Example: Two 2024 papers both citing the same 2020 dataset ‚Üí they're likely addressing similar questions
  • Use Case: Identify research fronts and emerging trends
Co-Citation (Looking Backward): Paper C (2024) ‚Üí cites ‚Üí Paper A (2015) + Paper B (2018) [Shows what PAST work influenced current research] Bibliographic Coupling (Looking Sideways): Paper A (2024) ‚Üí cites ‚Üí Paper X (2020) Paper B (2024) ‚Üí cites ‚Üí Paper X (2020) [Shows which CURRENT papers are related]

2. Keyword-Based Concepts:

üìå Co-word Analysis (Keyword Co-occurrence):

Analyzing keywords that appear together in the same documents.

  • Purpose: Map conceptual structure of a field
  • Example: "electric vehicle" and "charging infrastructure" appearing together 500 times ‚Üí strong thematic link
  • Types: Author keywords, Keywords Plus (ISI), index terms

3. Collaboration Analysis:

üìå Co-Authorship Networks:

Mapping collaboration patterns between researchers, institutions, or countries.

  • Author Level: Who collaborates with whom?
  • Institution Level: Which universities work together?
  • Country Level: International collaboration patterns

4. Important Metrics:

üìä h-index: A researcher has h-index of h if they have h papers with at least h citations each.

  • Example: h-index of 20 means 20 papers with ‚â•20 citations each
  • Balances productivity and impact

üìä Impact Factor (IF): Average citations per article in a journal over 2 years.

  • Formula: Citations(year N) to articles published in (year N-1 & N-2) / Total articles in (N-1 & N-2)
  • Used to rank journals, not individual papers

üìä Total Citations (TC): How many times a paper has been cited (all time).

üìä TC per Year: TC divided by years since publication (accounts for age).

1.4 Types of Bibliometric Studies

Performance Analysis vs. Science Mapping

Two Main Categories:

1Ô∏è‚É£ Performance Analysis (Descriptive)

Goal: Quantify research activity and impact

Questions Answered:

  • How many papers published per year? (Growth trends)
  • Who are the most productive authors?
  • Which journals publish the most?
  • Which papers are most cited?
  • Which countries are most active?
  • What's the collaboration index?

Tools: Biblioshiny (Dataset Overview, Sources, Authors, Documents tabs)

Outputs: Tables, bar charts, trend lines

2Ô∏è‚É£ Science Mapping (Relational)

Goal: Visualize relationships and structure

Questions Answered:

  • What are the main research themes?
  • How do themes evolve over time?
  • Which concepts are central vs. peripheral?
  • What are the research fronts vs. foundations?
  • How do authors/institutions collaborate?

Tools: VOSviewer, Biblioshiny (Conceptual Structure, Social Structure)

Outputs: Network maps, thematic maps, Sankey diagrams

Typical Study Structure:

  1. Performance Analysis First: Describe the dataset (who, what, when, where)
  2. Science Mapping Second: Analyze relationships (themes, evolution, networks)
  3. Interpretation Third: Explain the "why" behind the patterns

1.6 Strengths and Limitations

Critical perspective on bibliometrics

‚úÖ Advantages:

  • Objectivity: Reduces researcher bias through quantitative methods
  • Scalability: Can analyze thousands of papers efficiently
  • Reproducibility: Others can verify your results with same data and methods
  • Pattern Discovery: Reveals trends not visible in traditional reviews
  • Visual Communication: Network maps are compelling for presentations
  • Time-Efficient: Faster than reading 1000+ papers manually

‚ùå Limitations & Criticisms:

1. Citation Bias:

  • Older papers have more time to accumulate citations
  • English-language bias (non-English papers underrepresented)
  • Negative citations (citing to criticize) counted same as positive
  • Self-citations can inflate metrics

2. Database Limitations:

  • WoS and Scopus are not exhaustive (exclude many journals, books, conferences)
  • Paywalls limit access (need institutional subscription)
  • Indexing errors and missing metadata

3. Oversimplification Risk:

  • Quantitative metrics miss nuanced intellectual contributions
  • Can't assess quality of ideas, only their visibility
  • Gaming the system: citation cartels, strategic citing

4. Field Differences:

  • Citation practices vary by discipline (medical science cites more than math)
  • Can't compare h-index across fields directly

‚ö†Ô∏è Best Practice

Always combine bibliometric analysis with qualitative insights! Read the key papers in your clusters. Explain the "why" behind the numbers. Bibliometrics shows patterns; you provide interpretation.

1.7 When to Use Bibliometric Analysis

Is bibliometrics right for your research?

‚úÖ Use Bibliometrics When:

  • Your research field has substantial published literature (500+ papers)
  • You want to map the evolution of a research domain
  • You need to identify research gaps systematically
  • You're writing a systematic or scoping review
  • You want to justify your research topic with quantitative evidence
  • You need to identify key authors, journals, or institutions
  • Your supervisor/committee values mixed-methods approaches

‚ùå Don't Use Bibliometrics When:

  • Very small sample size (<100 papers) ‚Üí traditional review better
  • Very new field (publications from last 1-2 years only) ‚Üí not enough citation data
  • You need deep content analysis of theories ‚Üí qualitative review better
  • Limited database access and no alternative data sources
  • Your research question requires reading full texts (not just metadata)

üí° Hybrid Approach (Recommended):

Combine bibliometrics with traditional review:

  1. Phase 1 (Bibliometric): Map the field, identify clusters and key papers (1-2 weeks)
  2. Phase 2 (Traditional): Read and synthesize the most influential papers in each cluster (4-8 weeks)
  3. Phase 3 (Integration): Use quantitative findings to structure your qualitative narrative

2.1 Planning Your Search Strategy

Define your scope before searching

Step 1: Define Your Research Question

Be specific! A well-defined question leads to better search results.

‚ùå Too Broad:

"I want to study transportation."

‚úÖ Well-Defined:

"I want to analyze research trends in electric vehicle adoption in urban areas, focusing on consumer behavior and policy interventions, from 2015-2024."

Step 2: Identify Key Concepts

Break your research question into 2-4 main concepts:

  • Concept 1: Electric vehicles (main topic)
  • Concept 2: Adoption / Consumer behavior (aspect)
  • Concept 3: Urban areas / Cities (context - optional)

Step 3: Brainstorm Synonyms and Related Terms

List all possible ways authors might refer to each concept:

2.2 Mastering Boolean Operators & Wildcards

The secret to effective search queries

Boolean Operators:

1. OR (Expand Your Search):

Use OR to include synonyms and related terms. Either term will match.

"electric vehicle*" OR "battery electric vehicle*" OR "EV" OR "BEV" ‚Üí Finds papers mentioning ANY of these terms

2. AND (Narrow Your Search):

Use AND to combine different concepts. ALL concepts must be present.

("electric vehicle*" OR "EV") AND ("adopt*" OR "acceptance") ‚Üí Finds papers mentioning BOTH electric vehicles AND adoption

3. NOT (Exclude Terms):

Use NOT to exclude irrelevant results. Use sparingly!

"autonomous vehicle*" NOT "aircraft" ‚Üí Excludes papers about autonomous aircraft

Wildcards and Truncation:

* (Asterisk) - Multiple Character Wildcard:

Matches zero or more characters. Essential for word variations!

transport* ‚Üí matches: transport, transportation, transported, transporting sustain* ‚Üí matches: sustain, sustainable, sustainability adopt* ‚Üí matches: adopt, adoption, adopted, adopting

? (Question Mark) - Single Character Wildcard:

Matches exactly one character. Use for spelling variations.

behavio?r ‚Üí matches: behavior (US) and behaviour (UK) optimi?ation ‚Üí matches: optimization and optimisation

"Quotation Marks" - Phrase Search:

Searches for exact phrases. Words must appear together in that order.

"machine learning" ‚Üí finds only "machine learning" (not "learning machine") "climate change" ‚Üí exact phrase only

Combining Operators (Nesting):

Use parentheses ( ) to control the order of operations:

TITLE-ABS-KEY ( ("electric vehicle*" OR "EV" OR "battery electric vehicle*" OR "BEV") AND ("adopt*" OR "acceptance" OR "consumer behavior" OR "purchase intention") AND ("urban" OR "cit*" OR "metropolitan") )

‚ö†Ô∏è Common Mistakes:

  • Forgetting wildcards: "transport" misses "transportation"
  • Too narrow: Using AND too much ‚Üí very few results
  • Too broad: Using only OR ‚Üí millions of irrelevant results
  • Acronym ambiguity: "EV" might match "electric vehicle" but also "embedded value" ‚Üí check results!

2.3 Web of Science: Complete Walkthrough

From search to export

Step 1: Access Web of Science

  1. Go to your university library website
  2. Search for "Web of Science" in the database list
  3. Click to access (may require login with student credentials)
  4. You should see the WoS search interface

Step 2: Select Database Collection

In the dropdown menu, select: "Web of Science Core Collection"

This is the most comprehensive and includes citation data.

Step 3: Choose Search Fields

WoS offers different search fields:

  • Topic (Recommended): Searches Title, Abstract, Keywords, Keywords Plus
  • Title: Only in paper titles (too narrow usually)
  • Author: Search by author name
  • Publication Name: Search specific journals

üí° Recommendation: Use "Topic" for most bibliometric studies.

Step 4: Enter Your Search Query

Example query for electric vehicle adoption:

TS = ( ("electric vehicle*" OR "EV" OR "battery electric vehicle*" OR "BEV" OR "plug-in electric") AND ("adopt*" OR "acceptance" OR "consumer" OR "purchase" OR "uptake") )

Note: TS = Topic Search in WoS syntax

Step 5: Set Timespan

Specify your date range:

  • All years: Complete historical overview
  • Last 10 years: Recent trends (e.g., 2015-2024)
  • Custom range: Specific period of interest

üí° Tip: For emerging topics, use shorter timespan (5 years). For established fields, 10-20 years shows evolution.

Step 6: Apply Filters (Optional but Recommended)

After searching, refine results using left sidebar:

  • Document Types: Select "Article" and "Review" (exclude editorial, book chapters if desired)
  • Languages: Usually "English" for international analysis
  • Research Areas: Narrow to your field (e.g., "Engineering," "Environmental Sciences")
  • Source Titles: Include/exclude specific journals

Step 7: Review and Refine

Check a sample of results (first 10-20 papers). Ask yourself:

  • Are these papers relevant to my topic? ‚úÖ
  • Am I missing important papers I know about? ‚ùå ‚Üí Broaden search
  • Are there many irrelevant papers? ‚ùå ‚Üí Narrow search or add exclusions

Step 8: Export Data

CRITICAL STEP - Follow Exactly!

  1. Click checkbox to select records:
    • If ‚â§500 records: Select "All records on this page" (repeat for all pages)
    • If >500: Select 500 at a time, export multiple files
  2. Click "Export" button (top right)
  3. Choose export format: "Other File Formats"
  4. File format: "Tab-delimited (Win, UTF-8)"
  5. Record Content: "Full Record and Cited References" ‚ö†Ô∏è Essential!
  6. Records to export: "Records: 1 to 500" (or your range)
  7. Click "Export"
  8. Save file as savedrecs.txt (default name is fine)

‚ö†Ô∏è Critical: Why "Full Record and Cited References"?

This option includes:

  • All bibliographic metadata (authors, title, abstract, keywords)
  • Cited references - Required for co-citation analysis
  • Complete for all Biblioshiny analyses

Without this, you can't do co-citation or coupling analysis!

Step 9: Handle Large Datasets (>500 records)

WoS limits exports to 500-1000 records per batch:

  1. Export records 1-500 ‚Üí save as savedrecs1.txt
  2. Export records 501-1000 ‚Üí save as savedrecs2.txt
  3. Continue until all records exported
  4. Biblioshiny can merge multiple files automatically (we'll cover this in Module 3)

2.4 Scopus: Complete Walkthrough

Alternative/complementary to WoS

  1. Go to your university library ‚Üí Find "Scopus" database

Scopus syntax is slightly different from WoS:

Note: TITLE-ABS-KEY searches title, abstract, and keywords

Use left sidebar filters:

  1. Click "Select all" (up to 2000 records)

  3. Choose: "CSV Export"

2.5 Advanced Search Strategies

Pro techniques for better results

1. Iterative Search Refinement

Don't expect perfect results on first try! Use this workflow:

  1. Pilot search: Start broad, see what you get (check first 50 papers)
  2. Identify noise: What irrelevant papers appear? Add exclusions
  3. Find gaps: Missing key papers you know? Add more synonyms
  4. Iterate 2-3 times until results are ~80-90% relevant

2. Using Cited Reference Search (WoS Only)

Find papers that cited a seminal work:

  1. Click "Cited Reference Search" in WoS
  2. Enter author name or DOI of influential paper
  3. See all papers that cited it
  4. Useful for tracing research development from a specific starting point

3. Snowballing Technique

Expand search using references:

  • Backward snowballing: Check references of key papers ‚Üí find older foundational work
  • Forward snowballing: See who cited key papers ‚Üí find recent developments

4. Field-Specific Strategies

# Engineering/Technology: Include: patents, conference papers Databases: Scopus better coverage # Medical/Health Sciences: Include: PubMed, MEDLINE Use MeSH terms # Social Sciences: Broader keyword sets needed Consider Google Scholar as supplement

5. Testing Search Validity

How do you know your search is good? Check these:

  • ‚úÖ Includes known important papers: Search for 3-5 key papers by title. Are they in your results?
  • ‚úÖ Reasonable size: 200-5000 papers ideal. <100 too narrow, >10000 too broad
  • ‚úÖ Consistent with field growth: Check annual publication trend. Should increase or be stable, not random spikes
  • ‚úÖ Relevant abstracts: Read 20 random abstracts. Are >80% relevant?

2.6 Data Screening & Quality Control

Clean data = reliable results

PRISMA Flow Diagram

Document your selection process using PRISMA (Preferred Reporting Items for Systematic Reviews):

üì• Identification:

  • Records identified from WoS: n = 2,450
  • Records identified from Scopus: n = 3,100
  • Total before deduplication: n = 5,550

üîç Screening:

  • Duplicates removed: n = 1,200
  • Records screened: n = 4,350
  • Records excluded (irrelevant, wrong language, etc.): n = 650

‚úÖ Included:

  • Final dataset: n = 3,700 papers
  • Date range: 2015-2024
  • Document types: Articles (90%), Reviews (10%)

Inclusion/Exclusion Criteria

Define clear criteria before screening:

‚úÖ Inclusion Criteria (Example):

  • Peer-reviewed journal articles and reviews
  • Published in English
  • Published between 2015-2024
  • Focused on electric vehicle adoption, consumer behavior, or related policy
  • Empirical or conceptual studies

‚ùå Exclusion Criteria (Example):

  • Editorials, letters, book chapters, conference abstracts
  • Non-English publications
  • Papers focused solely on technical specifications (not adoption)
  • Duplicates
  • Retracted papers

Common Data Quality Issues

1. Duplicates:

  • Same paper appears multiple times (different databases or indexing errors)
  • Solution: Biblioshiny has built-in duplicate removal (we'll cover in Module 3)

2. Missing Metadata:

  • Some papers missing abstracts, keywords, or author information
  • Solution: Usually safe to keep if published in reputable journals. Manual check if critical

3. Inconsistent Author Names:

  • "Smith, J." vs. "Smith, John" vs. "Smith, J.A."
  • Solution: Biblioshiny attempts to standardize. For critical authors, manual disambiguation may be needed

4. Keyword Variations:

  • "machine-learning" vs. "machine learning" vs. "ML"
  • Solution: VOSviewer has thesaurus feature to merge similar keywords

üí° Quality Check Before Analysis:

  1. Open exported file in Excel/text editor - does it look complete?
  2. Check record count - matches what you expect?
  3. Scan a few random records - all fields populated?
  4. If using multiple files, ensure consistent format

2.7 Common Pitfalls & How to Avoid Them

Learn from others' mistakes

Top 10 Mistakes (And Solutions):

1. ‚ùå Exporting without "Cited References"

‚Üí ‚úÖ Always select "Full Record and Cited References" in WoS

2. ‚ùå Forgetting wildcards

‚Üí ‚úÖ Use "sustain*" not "sustainable" (misses "sustainability")

3. ‚ùå Search too narrow (only 50 papers)

‚Üí ‚úÖ Add more synonyms, broaden date range, include related concepts

4. ‚ùå Search too broad (50,000 papers)

‚Üí ‚úÖ Add more specific concepts with AND, apply filters, narrow timespan

5. ‚ùå Not documenting search strategy

‚Üí ‚úÖ Save exact search string, date accessed, filters used. You'll need this for the paper!

6. ‚ùå Using only one database

‚Üí ‚úÖ Cross-check WoS and Scopus for comprehensiveness

7. ‚ùå Ignoring duplicates

‚Üí ‚úÖ Always remove duplicates before analysis (inflates counts)

8. ‚ùå Mixing document types inconsistently

‚Üí ‚úÖ Decide upfront: articles + reviews only? Or include conference papers? Be consistent

9. ‚ùå Not testing search validity

‚Üí ‚úÖ Check if known important papers are in your results

10. ‚ùå Exporting wrong file format

‚Üí ‚úÖ WoS: Tab-delimited (.txt). Scopus: CSV (.csv). Check file extensions!

üéØ Pro Tip: Create a Search Log

Keep a document with:

  • Date of search
  • Database used
  • Exact search string
  • Filters applied
  • Number of results
  • File names exported

This becomes your "Data Collection" section in your paper!

4.6 Interpreting Networks - What Do They Mean?

From visualization to insights

Reading a Keyword Co-occurrence Network:

1. Identify Clusters (Colors):

  • Each color = a thematic cluster (research sub-topic)
  • Count clusters: "Our analysis revealed 5 major research themes..."
  • Name each cluster based on keywords within it

Example naming: Red cluster contains "battery", "charging", "infrastructure" ‚Üí Name it "Technology & Infrastructure"

2. Central vs. Peripheral Keywords:

  • Central (middle of network): Core concepts connecting multiple themes
  • Peripheral (edges): Specialized or emerging topics

3. Bridges Between Clusters:

  • Keywords connecting different clusters = interdisciplinary concepts
  • Example: "policy" appears between "technology" and "consumer behavior" clusters ‚Üí Policy mediates tech and adoption

4. Node Size:

  • Larger nodes = more frequent keywords = more researched topics
  • Identify: "The most prominent keywords were X, Y, Z..."

Writing About Networks in Your Paper:

"Keyword co-occurrence analysis (Figure 3) revealed five distinct research clusters. The largest cluster (Cluster 1, red) focused on technological aspects, prominently featuring 'battery technology', 'charging infrastructure', and 'range anxiety'. Cluster 2 (green) emphasized consumer perspectives, with keywords such as 'purchase intention', 'consumer acceptance', and 'perceived value'. The network structure indicates that 'policy incentives' serves as a bridge between technological and consumer-oriented research, suggesting policy's mediating role in EV adoption."

üí° Pro Tip:

Don't just describe what you see - interpret WHY it matters!

‚ùå Bad: "The network has 5 clusters."

‚úÖ Good: "The emergence of 5 distinct clusters reveals the field's evolution from purely technological focus (early years) to multidisciplinary integration of consumer behavior and policy research."

  • For researchers: Identified gaps, future directions
  • For practitioners/policymakers: Actionable insights

4.4 Limitations

  • Database limitations (WoS/Scopus not exhaustive)
  • English-language bias
  • Recency bias (recent papers have fewer citations)
  • Quantitative focus (complement with qualitative reading)

Conclusion (Separate Section):

  • Concise summary (3-4 paragraphs)
  • Restate contributions
  • Future research agenda (3-5 specific directions)
  • Closing statement (impact/significance)

5.6 Journal Selection Strategy

Where to submit?

Top Journals for Bibliometric Studies:

Multidisciplinary (Accept from any field):

  • Scientometrics (Q1, dedicated bibliometrics journal)
  • Journal of Informetrics (Q1)
  • Journal of the Association for Information Science and Technology (Q1)
  • PLOS ONE (Q2, open access, fast review)

Field-Specific (Topic must match journal scope):

  • Transportation: Transportation Research Part A/D, Transport Policy
  • Energy: Energy Policy, Applied Energy, Renewable & Sustainable Energy Reviews
  • Environment: Journal of Cleaner Production, Environmental Science & Policy
  • Engineering: Journal of Engineering & Technology Management
  • Business: Technological Forecasting & Social Change, Journal of Business Research

Selection Criteria:

  1. Scope match: Read journal's aims & scope. Does your topic fit?
  2. Check previous bibliometric papers: Search journal name + "bibliometric" in WoS. Do they publish bibliometric studies?
  3. Impact Factor / Quartile: Check Q1 or Q2 in your field (use Scimago or WoS Journal Citation Reports)
  4. Review speed: Check recent papers' submission-to-acceptance time
  5. Open Access option: Consider if you have funding for OA fees

Look at your "Most Relevant Sources" table from Biblioshiny. The top journals publishing on your topic are likely good targets for submission!

5.7 Writing Best Practices & Common Mistakes

Do's and Don'ts

‚úÖ DO:

  • Combine quant + qual: Read top papers in each cluster, cite them, explain themes
  • Use active voice: "We analyzed..." not "Was analyzed..."
  • Define acronyms first use: "Electric Vehicle (EV)"
  • Cite recent reviews: Show awareness of previous literature reviews
  • Be transparent: Report limitations honestly
  • Follow journal formatting: Download template early, use it from the start

‚ùå DON'T:

  • Don't just list numbers: "125 papers were published" ‚Üí Explain why that matters!
  • Don't overclaim: "This is the first study ever..." ‚Üí Probably not true, be humble
  • Don't ignore outliers: If something unexpected appears, discuss it!
  • Don't copy-paste from Biblioshiny: Rephrase, synthesize, interpret
  • Don't skip proofreading: Typos harm credibility

Common Reviewer Comments (How to Avoid):

"The paper is purely descriptive, lacks depth"

‚Üí Fix: Add qualitative interpretation. Read and cite key papers from your clusters.

"Methodology not reproducible"

‚Üí Fix: Provide exact search string, all parameters, software versions

"Results not novel"

‚Üí Fix: Compare with previous reviews, highlight what's NEW in your findings

"Figures are low quality or unclear"

‚Üí Fix: Export VOSviewer as PDF, ensure labels readable, add detailed captions
