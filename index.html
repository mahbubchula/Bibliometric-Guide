1. Fundamentals & Key Concepts

Citations, Co-citations, and Coupling

Bibliometric analysis uses statistical methods to analyze scientific publications. Key concepts include:

  • Citation Analysis: Who cites whom? Measures impact.
  • Co-citation Analysis: Two documents cited by a third. Measures relatedness of past work.
  • Bibliographic Coupling: Two documents citing the same third document. Measures relatedness of current work.
  • Co-word Analysis: Keywords appearing together. Maps conceptual structure.
üåç

2. Real-World Applications

Why do we do this?

  • Identify Trends: emerging topics vs. declining ones.
  • Map Collaboration: find potential partners or influential institutions.
  • Literature Review: Provide a quantitative backbone to your qualitative review.

1. Designing Search Queries

Boolean Operators & Wildcards

The quality of your analysis depends entirely on your data. Use Boolean operators to refine your search:

TITLE-ABS-KEY ( "traffic safety" OR "road safety" ) AND TITLE-ABS-KEY ( "artificial intelligence" OR "deep learning" )

Tip: Use * for wildcards (e.g., transport* matches transport, transportation, transporting).

üì•

2. Exporting Data (WoS & Scopus)

Getting the right format

Web of Science (WoS):

  1. Select records (up to 500/1000 at a time).
  2. Click Export > Tab Delimited File.
  3. Record Content: Choose "Full Record and Cited References" (Crucial!).

Scopus:

  1. Select all documents.
  2. Click Export > CSV.
  3. Select ALL available information (Citation, Bibliographical, Abstract, Keywords, etc.).

1. Installation & Setup

Getting R and RStudio ready

Run the following command in RStudio to install and launch Biblioshiny:

install.packages("bibliometrix") library(bibliometrix) biblioshiny()

This will open the web interface in your browser.

üìä

2. Core Analyses

Overview, Sources, Authors, Documents

  • Main Information: Summary of timespan, document types, and collaboration index.
  • Annual Scientific Production: Growth of the field over time.
  • Most Relevant Sources: Top journals publishing on this topic (Bradford's Law).
  • Most Cited Documents: The foundational papers in the field.
üï∏Ô∏è

3. Conceptual Structure

Thematic Maps & Evolution

Thematic Map: Plots clusters based on Density (development) and Centrality (relevance).

  • Motor Themes: High density, High centrality (Mainstream, developed).
  • Niche Themes: High density, Low centrality (Developed but isolated).
  • Emerging/Declining: Low density, Low centrality.
  • Basic Themes: Low density, High centrality (General, foundational).

1. Network Construction

Creating Maps from Bibliographic Data

Select "Create" > "Create a map based on bibliographic data".

  • Co-authorship: See research groups and country collaboration.
  • Co-occurrence: Keyword analysis to find topic clusters.
  • Citation: Direct citation links between documents/sources.

2. Customization & Styling

Making it Publication-Ready

  • Scale: Adjust node size based on citations or occurrences.
  • Colors: Use distinct color palettes (e.g., Viridis) for clusters.
  • Labels: Avoid overlap, set minimum strength to filter noise.
  • Export: Save as high-res PNG or vector (PDF/SVG) for best print quality.

Check the templates folder for the detailed VOSviewer guide.

1. Standard Paper Structure

IMRAD for Bibliometrics

  • Introduction: Background, research gap, specific research questions (RQs).
  • Methodology: Database choice, search string, inclusion/exclusion criteria (PRISMA).
  • Results:
    • Performance Analysis (Productivity, Citations)
    • Science Mapping (Co-word, Co-citation networks)
  • Discussion: Interpret the clusters, future research directions.
  • Conclusion: Summary and implications.
‚úÖ

2. Best Practices

Do's and Don'ts

  • DO: Combine quantitative stats with qualitative insight. Read the key papers in the clusters!
  • DON'T: Just list numbers. Explain why a trend is happening.
  • DO: Use the PRISMA flow diagram to show your selection process.
